## Targeted Sources
Sources matched to expected findings in an asset allocation risk-return-liquidity brief

### Markowitz, 1952
**Full Citation:** Markowitz, H. (1952). Portfolio selection. *The Journal of Finance*, *7*(1), 77–91. https://doi.org/10.2307/2975974
**Key Finding:** Investors should select portfolios based on the entire mean-variance efficient frontier rather than maximising expected return alone, formalising the risk-return tradeoff as the foundation of modern portfolio theory.
**Tags:** #modern-portfolio-theory | #mean-variance-optimization | #efficient-frontier
**Supports:** "Mean-variance optimisation identifies a set of efficient portfolios that maximise expected return for each level of risk."

### DeMiguel, Garlappi, & Uppal, 2009
**Full Citation:** DeMiguel, V., Garlappi, L., & Uppal, R. (2009). Optimal versus naive diversification: How inefficient is the 1/N portfolio strategy? *The Review of Financial Studies*, *22*(5), 1915–1953. https://doi.org/10.1093/rfs/hhn048
**Key Finding:** The equally-weighted (1/N) portfolio consistently matches or outperforms optimised portfolios out-of-sample because estimation error in expected returns, variances, and covariances dominates any theoretical gains from optimisation.
**Tags:** #modern-portfolio-theory | #diversification | #estimation-error
**Supports:** "Naive diversification strategies can outperform theoretically optimal portfolios due to sensitivity of mean-variance optimisation to input estimation errors."

### Brinson, Hood, & Beebower, 1986
**Full Citation:** Brinson, G. P., Hood, L. R., & Beebower, G. L. (1986). Determinants of portfolio performance. *Financial Analysts Journal*, *42*(4), 39–44. https://doi.org/10.2469/faj.v42.n4.39
**Key Finding:** Investment policy (strategic asset allocation) explains on average 93.6% of the variation in quarterly total returns for US pension plans, dwarfing the contributions of security selection and market timing.
**Tags:** #strategic-vs-tactical-allocation | #portfolio-performance-attribution
**Supports:** "Strategic asset allocation decisions are the dominant driver of portfolio return variability, not security selection or tactical tilts."

### Ibbotson & Kaplan, 2000
**Full Citation:** Ibbotson, R. G., & Kaplan, P. D. (2000). Does asset allocation policy explain 40, 90, or 100 percent of performance? *Financial Analysts Journal*, *56*(1), 26–33. https://doi.org/10.2469/faj.v56.n1.2327
**Key Finding:** Asset allocation policy explains about 90% of the variability of returns over time but only about 40% of the variation of returns across funds, clarifying a common misinterpretation of Brinson et al.
**Tags:** #strategic-vs-tactical-allocation | #portfolio-performance-attribution
**Supports:** "The proportion of performance explained by asset allocation depends on whether one is comparing returns over time or across portfolios."

### Asness, Frazzini, & Pedersen, 2012
**Full Citation:** Asness, C. S., Frazzini, A., & Pedersen, L. H. (2012). Leverage aversion and risk parity. *Financial Analysts Journal*, *68*(1), 47–59. https://doi.org/10.2469/faj.v68.n1.1
**Key Finding:** Because many investors are leverage-averse, low-risk assets offer higher risk-adjusted returns than high-risk assets; risk parity portfolios exploit this by leveraging low-volatility assets rather than concentrating in equities.
**Tags:** #risk-parity | #leverage-aversion | #low-volatility-anomaly
**Supports:** "Risk parity allocations deliver superior risk-adjusted returns by equalising risk contributions across asset classes rather than dollar-weighting toward equities."

### Qian, 2011
**Full Citation:** Qian, E. (2011). Risk parity and diversification. *The Journal of Investing*, *20*(1), 119–127. https://doi.org/10.3905/joi.2011.20.1.119
**Key Finding:** True portfolio diversification requires equalising risk contributions across assets; a traditional 60/40 portfolio is dominated by equity risk, meaning it is far less diversified than its dollar weights suggest.
**Tags:** #risk-parity | #diversification | #risk-budgeting
**Supports:** "A 60/40 stock-bond portfolio derives approximately 90% of its risk from equities despite only 60% of capital being allocated there."

### Amihud & Mendelson, 1986
**Full Citation:** Amihud, Y., & Mendelson, H. (1986). Asset pricing and the bid–ask spread. *Journal of Financial Economics*, *17*(2), 223–249. https://doi.org/10.1016/0304-405X(86)90065-6
**Key Finding:** Assets with higher bid-ask spreads (lower liquidity) earn higher average returns, establishing the existence of a liquidity premium that compensates investors for bearing transaction costs and illiquidity.
**Tags:** #liquidity-premia | #asset-pricing | #bid-ask-spread
**Supports:** "Illiquid asset classes command a return premium that compensates investors for higher trading costs and reduced ability to exit positions."

### Ang, Papanikolaou, & Westerfield, 2014
**Full Citation:** Ang, A., Papanikolaou, D., & Westerfield, M. M. (2014). Portfolio choice with illiquid assets. *Management Science*, *60*(11), 2737–2761. https://doi.org/10.1287/mnsc.2014.1986
**Key Finding:** Optimal allocation to illiquid assets is substantially lower than mean-variance analysis suggests because illiquidity constrains rebalancing; investors must account for lock-up periods and transaction costs when sizing illiquid positions.
**Tags:** #liquidity-premia | #portfolio-choice | #illiquidity-constraints
**Supports:** "Theoretical optimal allocations to illiquid assets (private equity, real estate, infrastructure) must be discounted relative to mean-variance solutions to account for rebalancing frictions."

### Black & Litterman, 1992
**Full Citation:** Black, F., & Litterman, R. (1992). Global portfolio optimization. *Financial Analysts Journal*, *48*(5), 28–43. https://doi.org/10.2469/faj.v48.n5.28
**Key Finding:** By combining market-equilibrium expected returns with investor views through a Bayesian framework, the Black-Litterman model produces more stable and intuitive portfolio weights than traditional mean-variance optimisation.
**Tags:** #modern-portfolio-theory | #Bayesian-portfolio-construction | #capital-market-assumptions
**Supports:** "Incorporating subjective investor views into an equilibrium starting point yields portfolio allocations that are more robust and less sensitive to estimation error than unconstrained mean-variance optimisation."

### Fama & French, 2015
**Full Citation:** Fama, E. F., & French, K. R. (2015). A five-factor asset pricing model. *Journal of Financial Economics*, *116*(1), 1–22. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jfineco.2014.10.010
**Key Finding:** A five-factor model adding profitability (RMW) and investment (CMA) factors to the original three-factor model captures average returns better and renders the value factor (HML) redundant for describing the cross-section of US stock returns.
**Tags:** #factor-based-allocation | #asset-pricing | #value-momentum-quality
**Supports:** "Factor exposures — market, size, value, profitability, and investment — explain a significant portion of cross-sectional return variation and should inform asset allocation decisions."

### Asness, Moskowitz, & Pedersen, 2013
**Full Citation:** Asness, C. S., Moskowitz, T. J., & Pedersen, L. H. (2013). Value and momentum everywhere. *The Journal of Finance*, *68*(3), 929–985. https://doi.org/10.1111/jofi.12021
**Key Finding:** Value and momentum premia are pervasive across eight diverse markets and asset classes (stocks, bonds, currencies, commodities), suggesting common underlying risk factors that can be exploited through factor-based allocation.
**Tags:** #factor-based-allocation | #value | #momentum | #cross-asset
**Supports:** "Value and momentum factors deliver positive premia across asset classes, providing a rationale for factor-based diversification that spans equities, fixed income, currencies, and commodities."

### Ilmanen, 2011
**Full Citation:** Ilmanen, A. (2011). *Expected returns: An investor's guide to harvesting market rewards*. Wiley.
**Key Finding:** Long-run expected returns across asset classes are driven by a handful of rewarded factors (value, carry, momentum, volatility, liquidity) and vary through time, requiring forward-looking capital market assumptions rather than simple historical extrapolation.
**Tags:** #capital-market-assumptions | #expected-returns | #factor-based-allocation | #risk-premia
**Supports:** "Capital market assumptions should reflect forward-looking estimates of risk premia rather than relying solely on historical average returns, which embed period-specific biases."

### Campbell & Viceira, 2002
**Full Citation:** Campbell, J. Y., & Viceira, L. M. (2002). *Strategic asset allocation: Portfolio choice for long-term investors*. Oxford University Press.
**Key Finding:** Optimal asset allocation differs substantially for long-horizon investors versus single-period investors because predictability in returns and the hedging demand for intertemporal risk create horizon-dependent portfolios.
**Tags:** #strategic-vs-tactical-allocation | #long-horizon-investing

### Ang, 2014
**Full Citation:** Ang, A. (2014). *Asset management: A systematic approach to factor investing*. Oxford University Press.
**Key Finding:** Factor investing provides a unifying framework for asset management — every portfolio's return can be decomposed into factor exposures, making factors the building blocks of allocation rather than traditional asset classes.
**Tags:** #factor-based-allocation | #asset-management

### Dimson, Marsh, & Staunton, 2021
**Full Citation:** Dimson, E., Marsh, P., & Staunton, M. (2021). *Credit Suisse global investment returns yearbook 2021*. Credit Suisse Research Institute.
**Key Finding:** Over 121 years of global data, equities have outperformed bonds and bills in every major market, but the equity risk premium has varied substantially across countries and time periods, cautioning against naive extrapolation of US-centric historical returns.
**Tags:** #capital-market-assumptions | #historical-returns | #equity-risk-premium

### Roncalli, 2013
**Full Citation:** Roncalli, T. (2013). *Introduction to risk parity and budgeting*. Chapman & Hall/CRC.
**Key Finding:** Risk budgeting generalises risk parity by allowing non-equal risk allocations; the mathematical framework connects ERC (equal risk contribution) portfolios to diversification measures and shows how constraints alter optimal risk budgets.
**Tags:** #risk-parity | #risk-budgeting | #portfolio-construction

### Sharpe, 1992
**Full Citation:** Sharpe, W. F. (1992). Asset allocation: Management style and performance measurement. *The Journal of Portfolio Management*, *18*(2), 7–19. https://doi.org/10.3905/jpm.1992.409394
**Key Finding:** Returns-based style analysis decomposes fund performance into passive asset class exposures and active selection returns, providing a powerful tool for understanding whether a manager's value-add comes from allocation or stock-picking.
**Tags:** #strategic-vs-tactical-allocation | #performance-attribution | #style-analysis

### Pastor & Stambaugh, 2003
**Full Citation:** Pastor, L., & Stambaugh, R. F. (2003). Liquidity risk and expected stock returns. *The Journal of Political Economy*, *111*(3), 642–685. https://doi.org/10.1086/374184
**Key Finding:** Stocks with higher sensitivity to aggregate liquidity fluctuations earn substantially higher expected returns (7.5% annually), establishing market-wide liquidity as a priced systematic risk factor distinct from individual-stock illiquidity.
**Tags:** #liquidity-premia | #systematic-risk | #asset-pricing

### Kinlaw, Kritzman, & Turkington, 2017
**Full Citation:** Kinlaw, W. B., Kritzman, M., & Turkington, D. (2017). *A practitioner's guide to asset allocation*. Wiley.
**Key Finding:** Effective asset allocation requires addressing regime changes, non-normal return distributions, and estimation error simultaneously; the book surveys robust methods including resampling, shrinkage estimation, and regime-switching models.
**Tags:** #portfolio-construction | #estimation-error | #practitioner-methods

### Franzoni, Nowak, & Phalippou, 2012
**Full Citation:** Franzoni, F., Nowak, E., & Phalippou, L. (2012). Private equity performance and liquidity risk. *The Journal of Finance*, *67*(6), 2341–2373. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1540-6261.2012.01788.x
**Key Finding:** A significant portion of private equity excess returns compensates for liquidity risk exposure; after adjusting for the liquidity factor, PE alpha is substantially reduced, implying the "illiquidity premium" accounts for much of PE's apparent outperformance.
**Tags:** #liquidity-premia | #private-equity | #alternative-assets
